The average software engineer leaves $50,000–$100,000 on the table over their career by not negotiating. Not because offers are fair — they're intentionally anchored low — but because most people freeze when they get an offer and say 'that sounds great.' This guide gives you the exact scripts, the timing, and the data to negotiate confidently.

## The Fundamental Truth: Every Tech Offer Is Negotiable

Hiring managers do not extend offers at the maximum their company can pay. They extend offers at a number they think you'll accept, or slightly above. The budget range for most roles is typically 15–30% wide — meaning a job that offers $120K could potentially offer $140K to the right candidate who pushes back professionally. This is not cynicism; it's how compensation bands work at every company.

The fear of 'losing the offer' by negotiating is almost entirely unfounded. In 20+ years of aggregate recruiter data, offer rescissions due to salary negotiation are extraordinarily rare — the company has already invested weeks recruiting you, running you through interview rounds, and getting internal approvals. They're not going to start over because you asked for more.

## Research First: Know Your Number Before Any Conversation

Never enter a salary conversation without anchoring on market data. Primary sources: Levels.fyi (most accurate for US big tech, has global data), LinkedIn Salary (good for broader market), Glassdoor (often undercounts because only dissatisfied people post), Payscale (useful for global/non-US markets). For startup equity: Carta's compensation benchmarks and AngelList salary data.

Build a range, not a point: find the 25th, 50th, and 75th percentile for your role, location, and experience level. Your ask should be at the 75th percentile or above — you negotiate down, not up. If you anchor at the 50th percentile, you'll end up at the 25th. The market data is your justification, not your gut feeling.

## The India Tech Salary Landscape: Real Numbers You Can Anchor On

Before you negotiate, you need **India-specific benchmarks** — not US data scaled down. Here's what the market actually pays in 2024-25:

**Service companies (TCS, Infosys, Wipro, Cognizant, Accenture):**
- Fresher (0-1 yr): **₹3.5–6 LPA**
- Mid-level (3-5 yr): **₹8–14 LPA**
- Senior (7-10 yr): **₹18–28 LPA**

**Product startups (unicorns like Razorpay, CRED, Zerodha, PhonePe):**
- Fresher: **₹15–25 LPA** (with ESOPs worth ₹5–15L over 4 years)
- Mid-level: **₹30–55 LPA**
- Senior: **₹60–110 LPA**

**FAANG India (Google, Microsoft, Amazon, Meta, Apple):**
- L3/SDE-1: **₹35–55 LPA total comp**
- L4/SDE-2: **₹55–90 LPA total comp**
- L5/SDE-3: **₹90 LPA–₹1.8 Cr total comp**

A frontend engineer with 4 years' experience at a unicorn should not accept anything less than ₹35 LPA in 2025. If you're at ₹18 LPA in a service company with the same skills, you're underpaid by 80%. [Benchmark your salary](/salary) against your exact role and city before any conversation — Bengaluru pays 15–25% more than Pune or Hyderabad for the same role.

## The Scripts: What to Say at Each Stage

When asked for salary expectations early in the process: 'I'd prefer to learn more about the full scope of the role before discussing compensation. Could we revisit this once we're both confident it's a mutual fit?' This delays anchoring until you have maximum leverage (after an offer).

When you receive the offer, don't respond immediately: 'Thank you so much — I'm really excited about this role. Can I take a couple of days to review the full details and get back to you?' This is always the right move. Never negotiate in the same breath as receiving the offer.

The negotiation call: 'I've been really excited about this opportunity since we first spoke. After reviewing the offer and doing some market research, I was hoping we could get to [target number]. Based on [your experience with X] and [market data showing Y], I believe that's in line with what this role commands in the current market. Is there flexibility there?'

## When You Have a Competing Offer (The Strongest Position)

A competing offer is the most powerful negotiation lever in existence. If you have one: 'I've received another offer for [amount] from [company type, doesn't have to be specific]. You're my first choice — I'd love to make this work. Is there any flexibility to get closer to that number?' This framing does three things: communicates competing interest (creates urgency), affirms your preference (keeps the relationship warm), and makes a specific ask.

Even an offer from a company you'd never take is leverage. An offer from a direct competitor to the company you're negotiating with is maximum leverage — companies strongly prefer to not lose candidates to competitors and will often match or beat an offer from a direct rival.

## Beyond Base Salary: The Full Compensation Picture

Base salary is only part of total compensation. At larger companies, stock (RSUs at public companies, options at startups) can equal or exceed base salary over a 4-year vest. The joining/signing bonus (often $10K–$50K for senior roles at big tech) is frequently more negotiable than base salary because it comes from a different budget. Performance bonus structure (percentage, target, and payment frequency) matters for mid-to-senior roles.

Benefits with real dollar value: home office stipend ($1K–$3K at remote-first companies), learning/conference budget ($1K–$5K/year), equity refresh schedule, and health insurance quality. When base salary is truly capped ('our band ends at X'), pivot to: 'Is there flexibility on the signing bonus or equity grant?' Companies often have more room in these buckets than in base salary.

## Decoding Indian CTC Structures: Don't Get Fooled by the Big Number

Indian companies are notorious for inflating CTC with components you'll never see in your bank account. A **₹25 LPA offer** can mean wildly different take-home depending on structure. Here's what to scrutinize:

**Fixed vs Variable:** If the offer is ₹25 LPA with 30% variable, your guaranteed pay is only ₹17.5 LPA. Variable pay at companies like Amazon India or Flipkart often pays out at 70–90% of target — ask for historical payout data.

**Retention bonuses:** Some companies (especially Accenture, Capgemini at senior levels) include a "joining bonus" of ₹3–5L that's clawed back if you leave in 12–24 months. This is not real money you can count on.

**ESOPs at startups:** A "₹40 LPA package with ₹20L ESOPs" sounds great until you realize the ESOPs vest over 4 years, the strike price might be high, and the company might never have a liquidity event. Discount ESOPs by 50–70% in your mental math unless it's a Series C+ company with clear IPO path.

**Gratuity, PF, insurance loading:** These often add ₹1–2L to your "CTC" but are not cash. Always ask: **"What's my monthly in-hand after tax?"** That's the only number that matters for your lifestyle.

When negotiating, push to **increase fixed cash and joining bonus**. Indian companies have surprising flexibility on **joining bonuses (₹2–10L is common)** and almost zero flexibility on fixed-base after a certain band.

## Negotiating a Raise at Your Current Company

The single best time to negotiate an internal raise is: when you've just delivered a major impact (shipped a project, saved costs, led a team), during performance review cycles (company has budget allocated), or when you have an external offer (this remains the most powerful lever internally too).

The internal negotiation script: 'I've been thinking about my compensation as I look ahead to the next year. Based on the work I delivered on [project] and [market research showing X for my role/level], I think there's a gap between my current comp and where the market is. I'd like to discuss getting to [number].' Follow up in writing after the conversation — it shows seriousness and creates a paper trail.

If the answer is no: get a specific timeline for when it could be revisited, and start interviewing externally. The external offer either converts to an internal raise or confirms it's time to move. Statistically, switching companies for a raise produces 10–20% increases while internal raises average 3–5% — the math strongly favors job movement for compensation growth.

## Common Negotiation Mistakes That Cost Indian Candidates Lakhs

**Mistake 1: Sharing current CTC upfront.** Recruiters at TCS, Infosys, and even product companies will ask "What's your current CTC?" in the first call. Legally, you don't have to disclose. Say: *"I'd prefer to discuss the value I bring to this role rather than anchor on my past compensation. What's the budgeted range for this position?"* If forced, share your **expected CTC**, not current.

**Mistake 2: Accepting the first offer because "it's already a 40% hike."** A 40% jump from ₹12 LPA to ₹17 LPA sounds great until you realize the market for your role is ₹24 LPA. Hike percentage is a vanity metric. **Absolute market rate** is what matters.

**Mistake 3: Negotiating over email when stakes are high.** Email lets recruiters reject in 30 seconds with a templated "we don't have flexibility." A 15-minute call lets you read tone, build rapport, and pivot to other levers (bonus, ESOPs, designation). Always request a call for the main negotiation.

**Mistake 4: Not negotiating the notice period or joining date.** If you're stuck in a 90-day notice and the new company wants you in 30, that gap is leverage — they may pay a **notice buyout (₹2–5L)** or a higher joining bonus to bridge it.

**Mistake 5: Going in without a strong resume.** If your resume is generic and your interview performance was just okay, you have minimal leverage. [Check your ATS score](/resume) before you even apply, and [practice interviews](/interview) so you're the candidate they're fighting to close, not one of three options.

The negotiation landscape has shifted dramatically in the last 18 months. Candidates now walk into salary conversations armed with **AI-generated comp analysis**, real-time scraped data from Levels.fyi and AmbitionBox, and ChatGPT-drafted negotiation scripts customized to the recruiter's company.

**What's actually working in 2025:**
- Using AI to draft a **personalized negotiation email** that references the specific JD, the interviewer's LinkedIn background, and 3 publicly known comp data points for that company

## FAQ

### Should I disclose my current salary when asked in an Indian interview?

You're not legally required to share your current CTC in India, though most recruiters ask aggressively. The smartest play: deflect to **expected CTC** instead. Say something like *"My current compensation isn't really relevant to the value I'd bring here — I'm looking for ₹X based on market rates for this role and my experience."* If pushed hard (some recruiters won't move forward without it), share a number that's already inflated by including your variable, ESOPs, and any retention components. Never share just your base. Companies use your current CTC to cap your offer at a 20–30% hike, regardless of what the role actually pays in the market.

### How much hike should I ask for when switching jobs in India?

The standard "30% hike" rule is outdated for tech in 2025. **Realistic ranges:** Service-to-service switch: 25–40%. Service-to-product: 60–120% (yes, this is real — a ₹14 LPA TCS engineer can credibly target ₹28–32 LPA at a product startup). Product-to-product lateral: 30–50%. Product-to-FAANG: 40–80%. The key isn't the hike percentage — it's the **absolute target number based on market data** for your role, years of experience, and city. If your current employer underpaid you, your hike percentage will look huge but you're just catching up to market. Anchor on the destination, not the delta.

### What if the recruiter says 'this is our final offer, take it or leave it'?

This is almost always a negotiation tactic, not a fact. Respond calmly: *"I understand there are budget constraints. I'm genuinely excited about this role, so I want to make this work. If base isn't flexible, could we look at the joining bonus, ESOP grant, or an earlier performance review at 6 months instead of 12?"* This pivots from a dead-end to multiple new doors. About 70% of "final offers" have flexibility in non-base components. If they truly won't budge anywhere, ask for a **written 6-month review commitment with a specific raise target** — that locks in your next increase before you even join.

### When should I walk away from a negotiation?

Walk away if: (1) The offer is significantly below market (more than 25% under benchmarks) and they refuse to move on base, bonus, or equity. (2) The recruiter becomes hostile or threatening during negotiation — this signals the company culture and you'll face the same behavior at promotion time. (3) The role itself is misaligned with your career goals and you were only chasing the comp. (4) You have a stronger offer in hand that's better on both money and growth. Walking away is also a negotiation move — politely declining an offer often triggers a "let me check with leadership" call within 48 hours with a revised number. But never bluff a walk-away you're not prepared to follow through on.

## Bottom Line

- **Every offer is negotiable** — recruiters expect pushback and budget ranges are 15–30% wide. Not negotiating costs ₹5–15L per job switch.
- **Research before you talk** — anchor on Levels.fyi, AmbitionBox, and [salary benchmarks](/salary) for your exact role, city, and experience level. Aim for the 75th percentile.
- **Decode Indian CTC carefully** — fixed cash, variable payout history, and joining bonus matter more than the headline "LPA" number. Always calculate monthly in-hand.
- **Competing offers are your strongest lever** — even one alternative offer typically unlocks 15–25% more. Always interview at 3–5 places in parallel.
- **Switching beats staying for comp growth** — internal raises average 8–12% in India, while job switches yield 30–60%+. The math is brutal but clear: move every 2–3 years until you hit your target band.
